\section*{Scenario 4: Redistribution, the Lorenz Curve, and the Gini Coefficient}

A provincial government in Canada is concerned about rising income inequality and cost-of-living pressures. Similar to real-world debates about the Canada Child Benefit, refundable tax credits, and provincial affordability payments, the government considers a targeted redistribution policy. The policy gives a direct transfer to the lowest-income household and finances it through a tax on the highest-income household.

Suppose a small sample of five households has the following annual disposable incomes before the policy:

\[
25{,}000,\quad 35{,}000,\quad 50{,}000,\quad 85{,}000,\quad 205{,}000.
\]

The government introduces a redistribution policy that transfers \(\$15{,}000\) from the richest household to the poorest household. After the policy, the income distribution becomes:

\[
40{,}000,\quad 35{,}000,\quad 50{,}000,\quad 85{,}000,\quad 190{,}000.
\]

For Lorenz curve calculations, incomes should be ordered from lowest to highest after redistribution.

\bigskip

\textbf{Use the following discrete Gini coefficient formula:}

\[
G = 1 - \sum_{i=1}^{n}(Y_i + Y_{i-1})(X_i - X_{i-1}),
\]

where \(X_i\) is the cumulative population share and \(Y_i\) is the cumulative income share.




\item After the redistribution policy, total income will:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item Rise because the poorest household receives more income
\item Fall because taxation reduces incentives to work
\item Remain unchanged because the transfer is financed within the sample
\item Double because both tax and transfer are counted
\item Become zero because income is redistributed
\end{enumerate}

\item Before redistribution, the income share of the richest household is closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 31.25\%
\item 41.25\%
\item 51.25\%
\item 61.25\%
\item 71.25\%
\end{enumerate}

\item Before redistribution, the income share of the bottom 40\% of households is closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 10\%
\item 15\%
\item 20\%
\item 25\%
\item 30\%
\end{enumerate}
\newpage
\item After redistribution, the correctly ordered income distribution is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item \(40{,}000,\;35{,}000,\;50{,}000,\;85{,}000,\;190{,}000\)
\item \(35{,}000,\;40{,}000,\;50{,}000,\;85{,}000,\;190{,}000\)
\item \(25{,}000,\;35{,}000,\;50{,}000,\;85{,}000,\;205{,}000\)
\item \(15{,}000,\;35{,}000,\;50{,}000,\;85{,}000,\;220{,}000\)
\item \(40{,}000,\;50{,}000,\;35{,}000,\;190{,}000,\;85{,}000\)
\end{enumerate}

\item After redistribution, the income share of the richest household is closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 37.5\%
\item 42.5\%
\item 47.5\%
\item 52.5\%
\item 57.5\%
\end{enumerate}

\item Before redistribution, the cumulative income shares are closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item \(0,\;0.0625,\;0.1500,\;0.2750,\;0.4875,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.1000,\;0.2000,\;0.3000,\;0.5000,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.0500,\;0.1250,\;0.2500,\;0.5000,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.1250,\;0.2500,\;0.3750,\;0.6250,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.2500,\;0.3500,\;0.5000,\;0.7500,\;1.0000\)
\end{enumerate}

\item Before redistribution, using the discrete Lorenz curve formula, the Gini coefficient is closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 0.18
\item 0.28
\item 0.38
\item 0.48
\item 0.58
\end{enumerate}

\item After redistribution, the cumulative income shares are closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item \(0,\;0.0875,\;0.1875,\;0.3125,\;0.5250,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.1000,\;0.2000,\;0.3000,\;0.5000,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.0625,\;0.1500,\;0.2750,\;0.4875,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.1250,\;0.2125,\;0.3375,\;0.5250,\;1.0000\)
\item \(0,\;0.2000,\;0.3000,\;0.4250,\;0.6250,\;1.0000\)
\end{enumerate}

\item After redistribution, the Gini coefficient is closest to:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 0.20
\item 0.30
\item 0.40
\item 0.50
\item 0.60
\end{enumerate}

\item The redistribution policy changes inequality because:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item It raises total income and therefore automatically reduces inequality
\item It lowers total income and therefore automatically increases inequality
\item It increases the income share of lower-income households and lowers the income share of the richest household
\item It changes only the median income and leaves the Lorenz curve unchanged
\item It makes every household receive the same income
\end{enumerate}

\item The Lorenz curve after redistribution will:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item Move farther away from the 45-degree equality line
\item Move closer to the 45-degree equality line
\item Become vertical
\item Become horizontal
\item Remain exactly unchanged
\end{enumerate}

\item Suppose instead that the area under the Lorenz curve rises from \(0.38\) to \(0.42\). Using the relationship
\[
G = 1 - 2A,
\]
where \(A\) is the area under the Lorenz curve, the new Gini coefficient is:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item 0.58
\item 0.52
\item 0.42
\item 0.32
\item 0.16
\end{enumerate}
\newpage
\item Which statement best connects this scenario to real-world Canadian inequality policy?
\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
\item Redistribution policies can reduce measured inequality, but their effect depends on size, targeting, and financing
\item Redistribution always increases total GDP immediately
\item Redistribution has no effect on income distribution
\item Transfers only affect inflation and not inequality
\item The Gini coefficient cannot be used to evaluate redistribution
\end{enumerate}
