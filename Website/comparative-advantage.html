The Core Idea

Focus on what you give up,

🏆

Absolute Advantage

A country has an absolute advantage when it can produce more of a good using the same resources as another country. But this alone doesn't determine trade.

⚖️

Comparative Advantage

A country has a comparative advantage when it produces a good at a lower opportunity cost than another — even if it's less efficient in absolute terms.

🤝

The Insight

Both countries gain by specialising in what they do at the lowest relative cost, then trading. Total output rises for everyone.

A Simple Example

Two countries, two goods. Workers needed to produce one unit:

Country 🌾 Wheat (workers/unit) 🚗 Cars (workers/unit)
🇺🇸 United States 1 worker 2 workers
🇧🇷 Brazil 3 workers 4 workers

The US is more efficient at both goods — it has absolute advantage in both. Does Brazil have any reason to trade? Yes. Look at opportunity costs →

When both countries specialise and trade, total world output increases. Each nation consumes beyond what it could produce alone — a win-win created purely by comparative advantage.

Why It Matters

Shapes the Global Economy

1

Foundation of Free Trade

Comparative advantage is the economic rationale behind free trade agreements like NAFTA/USMCA and the WTO — nations liberalise trade because everyone can benefit.

2

Drives Global Supply Chains

Why does Germany dominate precision engineering while Bangladesh leads in textiles? Each has a different comparative advantage based on labour, capital, and skills.

3

Limits of the Theory

It assumes no transport costs, fixed resources, and perfect competition. In reality, strategic industries, inequality, and environmental costs complicate the picture.
