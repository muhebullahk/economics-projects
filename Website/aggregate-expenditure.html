How total planned spending determines equilibrium output — and why the economy can get stuck below full employment.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Building Blocks

AE = C + I + G + NX

AE= C+ I+ G+ NX
C

Consumption

Household spending on goods & services. Largest component (~60% of GDP). Driven by income, wealth, and expectations.

I

Investment

Business spending on capital and housing investment. Most volatile component — sensitive to interest rates & expectations.

G

Gov't Spending

Government purchases of goods & services. Does not include transfers (EI, pensions) — those affect C indirectly.

NX

Net Exports

Exports minus imports (X − M). Positive when Canada sells more abroad than it buys. Affected by exchange rates & foreign income.

Equilibrium condition: The economy is in equilibrium when actual output Y equals planned aggregate expenditure AE. When AE > Y, output expands; when AE < Y, output contracts.

The Consumption Function

C = a + bY — the relationship between household income and spending.

a Autonomous consumption — spending that occurs even at zero income, financed by savings or borrowing. The y-intercept of the C line.
b Marginal propensity to consume (MPC) — the fraction of each extra dollar of income spent on consumption. The slope of the C line.
MPC If MPC = 0.75: each extra $1 of income → $0.75 more consumption. Remaining $0.25 is saved (MPS = 1 − MPC = 0.25).
Key The C function slopes upward but less steeply than the 45° line — households never spend 100% of income (MPC < 1).

The Keynesian Cross

Equilibrium output is where planned spending AE equals actual output Y — where the AE line crosses the 45° line.

45° line Every point on this line satisfies AE = Y. It represents all possible equilibria — the economy must end up somewhere on it.
AE line Slope = MPC. Starts above zero (autonomous spending). Less steep than 45° because MPC < 1.
Y* Equilibrium output — where AE = Y. If Y < Y*, planned spending exceeds output → firms expand. If Y > Y*, output exceeds spending → firms cut back.

Recessionary & Inflationary Gaps

Equilibrium Y* need not equal potential output Yf. When they differ, the economy faces a gap that policy must close.

📉 Recessionary Gap

Y* < Yf — the AE line crosses the 45° line to the left of potential output.

Consequence: unemployment above the natural rate. Firms are producing less than the economy is capable of. Wages and prices may fall slowly, but not quickly enough.

Fix: shift AE upward — via government spending ↑, taxes ↓, or interest rates ↓ — until Y* rises to Yf.

→ Expansionary fiscal or monetary policy

📈 Inflationary Gap

Y* > Yf — the AE line crosses the 45° line to the right of potential output.

Consequence: the economy is overheating. Demand exceeds productive capacity. Firms cannot expand output, so prices rise — inflation accelerates.

Fix: shift AE downward — via government spending ↓, taxes ↑, or interest rates ↑ — until Y* falls back to Yf.

→ Contractionary fiscal or monetary policy
Key Keynesian insight: The economy does not automatically return to Yf. Without policy intervention, it can remain stuck in a recessionary gap indefinitely — this is the core argument for active macroeconomic policy.

The Multiplier Effect

A shift in autonomous spending raises the AE line — and the new equilibrium rises by more than the initial injection.

Shift Government increases spending by ΔG = 20. The entire AE line shifts up by 20 — AE₁ starts 20 above AE₀ at every income level.
New Y* The new equilibrium Y₁* is higher than Y₀*. But the increase ΔY = Y₁* − Y₀* is larger than ΔG = 20.
Formula Multiplier = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC). If MPC = 0.6: multiplier = 2.5. So ΔG = 20 → ΔY = 50.
Why Each round of spending becomes someone else's income. They spend a fraction (MPC), creating another round — and so on until the series converges.
