Prospect
Theory

How people actually evaluate gains, losses, and risk — replacing expected utility with a descriptive model that fits the data.

Kahneman & Tversky (1979)

Prospect Theory rests on four departures from expected utility.

📍

Reference Dependence

Outcomes are evaluated as gains or losses relative to a reference point — not as final wealth levels. The same outcome can feel like a gain or a loss.

⚖️

Loss Aversion

Losses loom larger than equivalent gains. The pain of losing $100 is roughly twice the pleasure of gaining $100. The value function is steeper in losses.

📉

Diminishing Sensitivity

Marginal impact falls as outcomes move further from the reference point — in both the gain and loss domains. The value function curves away from the origin.

Reference Dependence

People do not evaluate outcomes in terms of total wealth. They evaluate changes relative to where they currently are.

Core The reference point is typically the status quo — your current income, wealth, or health. Outcomes above it are gains; outcomes below it are losses.
Context A $500 bonus feels like a gain if you expected nothing, but a loss if you were promised $1,000. The objective amount is the same; only the reference point differs.
Endowment Endowment effect: once you own something, giving it up is experienced as a loss — so you demand more to sell it than you would pay to buy it.
Implication Standard welfare analysis based on final states is incomplete. How an outcome is framed — as a gain or a loss — matters independently of its objective value.

Loss Aversion & the Value Function

The value function is the signature diagram of Prospect Theory — an S-shaped curve that is steeper in losses than in gains.

Law Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains. Losing $100 hurts about as much as gaining $200 feels good. Coefficient λ ≈ 2.25.
Shape Concave above the reference point (risk aversion in gains). Convex below it (risk seeking in losses). Together, this produces the S-shape.
Evidence People reject 50/50 gambles of losing $100 or gaining $X unless X ≈ $200. The potential gain must be roughly double the potential loss to be acceptable.

Probability Weighting

People do not use objective probabilities. They transform them through a weighting function — overweighting small probabilities and underweighting large ones.

Small p A 1% chance of winning feels more significant than it should. This drives demand for lottery tickets and disproportionate fear of rare catastrophes.
Large p A 99% chance feels less than certain. People prefer a sure outcome over a 99% chance of something slightly better — the certainty effect.
Shape The weighting function w(p) is an inverted-S: it rises steeply near 0 and near 1, but is relatively flat in the middle range of probabilities.
Allais The Allais Paradox is explained by probability weighting: people overweight the certainty of one option relative to a near-certain alternative — violating EUT.

Diminishing Sensitivity

The marginal impact of a change decreases as you move further from the reference point — in both the gain and the loss domain.

Gains The difference between $0 and $100 feels larger than the difference between $900 and $1,000 — even though both are exactly $100. Gains show concavity.
Losses The difference between losing $0 and $100 feels larger than the difference between losing $900 and $1,000. Losses show convexity (risk seeking).
Combined Together, diminishing sensitivity and loss aversion generate the S-shaped value function: curved on both sides of the reference point, steeper on the loss side.
Policy Segregate gains (present each gain separately) but integrate losses (present them as one total). People feel better when gains are spread out and losses are bundled.
REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Finding vs Losing $20

You find $20 on the street — nice surprise, small happiness. You lose $20 from your pocket — awful feeling, ruins your day.

Same amount of money, but the loss feels roughly twice as bad as the gain feels good. This is loss aversion — and it explains why people hold losing investments too long, hate pay cuts more than they love pay raises, and cling to the status quo.
