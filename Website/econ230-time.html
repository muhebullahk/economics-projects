& Present Bias

Why good intentions fail — and why the future self cannot always trust the present self.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

Exponential vs. Hyperbolic Discounting

Standard economics assumes constant discounting. But people discount the near future far more steeply than the distant future — producing time-inconsistent preferences.

Standard Exponential discounting: value declines by the same percentage each period. Preferences are consistent — the same choice looks the same from any vantage point.
Actual Hyperbolic discounting: value drops sharply in the near term, then flattens. The near future is over-penalised relative to the distant future.
Problem Time inconsistency: you prefer $110 next week to $100 today — but when next week arrives, you prefer $100 immediately to waiting another week.
Result Plans made today are not carried out tomorrow. The self that plans and the self that acts face different preference orderings — generating self-control failures.

The β-δ Model

Quasi-hyperbolic discounting captures present bias with two parameters: δ for long-run patience and β for the bias toward the present.

δ (delta) The standard per-period discount factor — how much you discount one period into the future. Applies equally to all future periods. Measures long-run patience.
β (beta) An additional discount applied to everything that is not immediate. If β < 1, the present is over-weighted relative to all future periods.
Example β = 0.7 means a reward arriving next month is valued at only 70% of what it would be worth if it arrived today — beyond the standard δ discounting.
Naïve vs Sophisticated Naïve agents do not anticipate their own future bias and make promises they cannot keep. Sophisticated agents foresee it — and seek commitment devices.
Real-World Footprint

Present bias leaves a wide empirical trace.

🏦

Retirement Savings

People consistently plan to save more "next year" — but next year never comes. They repeatedly delay. Auto-enrolment in pension plans works precisely because it removes the need for an active decision.

💳

Credit Card Debt

Consumers borrow at high rates for present consumption, planning to pay off balances quickly. Balances accumulate. The average borrower carries debt at rates they would never accept if asked in advance.

🥦

Diet & Exercise

Ambitious Monday plans are abandoned by Tuesday. Immediate effort costs are over-weighted relative to future health benefits. The present self consistently overrules the planning self.

The Solution

Commitment Devices

What They Are

Mechanisms that restrict future choice to enforce a plan made in the present. The present self ties the future self's hands — preventing the future self from giving in to present bias.

Classic Example

Ulysses tied himself to the mast to resist the Sirens. Automatic pension contributions remove monthly decisions. Pre-commitment savings accounts lock funds until a target date.

Who Uses Them

Sophisticated agents — those who foresee their own future bias — actively seek commitment devices. Naïve agents do not anticipate bias and make promises they cannot keep.

Policy Design

Opt-out pension enrolment raises participation from ~40% to over 90%. Deadlines with penalties help procrastinators. Sin taxes on cigarettes and junk food act as commitment mechanisms.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

The New Year's Resolution

January 1st: You're pumped. "I'll go to the gym every day!" Your future self sounds incredibly disciplined.

February 1st: The alarm goes off at 6:30am. It's cold. The couch is right there.

You didn't change your values — you still believe exercise matters. But the immediate cost beats the distant benefit. That's present bias. The planning self and the acting self want different things.

"
Present bias explains why good intentions are not enough. The present self discounts the future too steeply — and knowing you should save, exercise, or quit is not the same as doing it. The solution is not willpower. It is better design.
