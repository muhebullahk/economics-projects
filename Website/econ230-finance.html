The Bridge

Individual biases can aggregate into systematic, market-wide patterns.

🧩

From Psychology to Prices

Heuristics and biases do not cancel out in markets — they can reinforce each other. When many investors share the same cognitive error, that error moves prices in ways that can persist for extended periods.

⚖️

The Efficient Market Hypothesis

The standard benchmark: prices reflect all available information, so sustained mispricings cannot exist. Behavioural finance challenges this by documenting systematic anomalies that EMH cannot explain.

🚧

Limits to Arbitrage

Even when rational traders identify a mispricing, they may be unable or unwilling to correct it. Risk, short-selling constraints, and investor horizons can all prevent arbitrage from restoring prices to fundamentals.

Asset Bubbles & Herding

Prices can deviate far from fundamental value for years. Herding — following others rather than independent information — is a key mechanism that sustains and amplifies these deviations.

Herding Investors observe the actions of others and infer information from them. When enough investors follow this strategy simultaneously, prices can move not on fundamentals but on the shared belief that prices will rise.
Dot-Com The NASDAQ rose ~400% between 1995 and March 2000, driven by enthusiasm for internet firms with no earnings. From peak to trough it fell 78%. Many firms valued in the billions had no revenue at all.
Housing 2008 US house prices rose 124% between 1997 and 2006. The belief that prices could only rise was itself the source of demand sustaining the rise — a classic self-reinforcing bubble narrative.
Greater Fool A rational investor can participate in a bubble if they expect to sell before it bursts. This dynamic — profiting by selling to someone who will pay even more — can sustain prices above fundamentals indefinitely.
Price Dynamics

Markets overreact to some information and underreact to other information.

Overreaction

Investors give too much weight to recent dramatic information. Stocks with strong recent performance become overvalued; stocks with poor recent performance become undervalued. Prices overshoot fundamentals.

Mean Reversion

De Bondt and Thaler (1985) showed past extreme losers outperform past extreme winners over 3–5 year horizons — consistent with overreaction followed by correction. Contrarian strategies exploit this pattern.

Underreaction

Markets are slow to incorporate certain information. Post-earnings announcement drift shows prices continuing to move in the direction of an earnings surprise for weeks or months — long after the news is public.

Momentum

In the short run (3–12 months), winners tend to keep winning and losers keep losing — consistent with underreaction. Momentum and reversal coexist because each operates on a different time horizon.

Noise Traders vs Rational Traders

DeLong, Shleifer, Summers, and Waldmann showed that noise traders — who trade on sentiment rather than information — can survive in equilibrium and permanently move prices away from fundamentals.

Noise Traders Trade on sentiment, tips, and trends — not on fundamental information. Individually they may lose money, but collectively they move prices and create risk that deters rational arbitrageurs from correcting those prices.
Rational Traders Know the fundamental value and want to arbitrage mispricings. But they face noise trader risk: the mispricing may worsen before it corrects, forcing losses if capital is withdrawn before the correction occurs.
Limits to Arbitrage Shleifer and Vishny: arbitrage requires capital and time. If a mispricing worsens, rational traders may face investor redemptions and be forced to exit at a loss — "arbitrage is not a reliable correcting mechanism."
Implication Markets can be systematically mispriced for extended periods. Prices reflect both fundamental information and investor sentiment, and sentiment can be the dominant force — especially in the short run.
Investor Sentiment

Optimism and pessimism beyond what fundamentals justify move prices in predictable ways.

What Is Sentiment?

The tendency of investors to form beliefs about future returns not fully justified by fundamentals. High-sentiment periods feature excessive optimism about earnings growth; low-sentiment periods feature excessive pessimism.

Measuring Sentiment

Indicators include IPO volume and first-day returns, closed-end fund discounts, mutual fund flows, surveys of investor confidence, and put-call ratios. Each captures a different dimension of collective market mood.

Effects on Returns

Baker and Wurgler showed high investor sentiment predicts lower future returns — especially for stocks hardest to value and arbitrage: small caps, growth stocks, young firms, and high-volatility stocks.

Extrapolation

Investors project recent returns into the future. After a long bull market, expectations become too optimistic; after a crash, too pessimistic. This amplifies boom-bust cycles rather than damping them — the opposite of what EMH predicts.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

GameStop 2021

A struggling video game retailer worth ~$20/share shot to ~$480 in two weeks — then crashed back down.

Reddit users piled in. Not because they believed in GameStop's business, but because everyone else was buying. Classic herding. Classic greater fool.

Sentiment and crowd psychology drove a $30B valuation for a company losing money. That's behavioural finance in the real world.
