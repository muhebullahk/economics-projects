Frontier

How scarcity forces trade-offs — and what the shape of a curve reveals about the cost of every choice.

The Core Idea

All possible combinations
with your current resources.

🔒

Fixed Resources

An economy has a finite amount of land, labour, and capital at any moment. The PPF shows every combination of two goods it can produce using all of those resources efficiently.

↔️

The Boundary

Points on the curve are fully efficient. Points inside the curve are inefficient — resources are wasted or idle. Points outside are currently unattainable.

💡

Why It Matters

The PPF makes scarcity visible. Every move along the frontier comes at a cost — producing more of one good always means producing less of another.

Reading the Frontier

Two goods: Food and Clothing. The curve traces every fully efficient combination. Its shape, and every point around it, carries meaning.

A — Efficient. On the frontier. All resources in use, nothing wasted.
B — Inefficient. Inside the curve. Idle workers, unused capacity.
C — Unattainable. Outside the curve. Impossible with current resources.
The Key Insight

The Slope Is the Opportunity Cost

Moving Along the Frontier
⬆ +10 units of Food −6 Clothing
⬆ +10 units of Clothing −10 Food

Every move along the frontier requires giving something up. There is no free lunch — only trade-offs.

Reading Opportunity Cost
1 unit of Food costs… 0.6 Clothing
1 unit of Clothing costs… 1⅔ Food

Opportunity cost = units of the forgone good ÷ units of the gained good. The PPF makes this ratio visible.

Key rule: The slope of the PPF at any point equals the opportunity cost of producing one more unit of the good on the horizontal axis — measured in units of the vertical-axis good foregone.

Why the Curve Bows Outward

A straight-line PPF assumes constant opportunity cost. In reality, resources are not perfectly interchangeable — and that changes everything.

🌾

First Transfers Are Cheap

Shift the first few workers from clothing to food and you pick those best suited to farming. Output of food rises a lot; you give up little clothing. Opportunity cost is low.

📈

Later Transfers Cost More

Keep shifting workers and you pull those who are really better at textiles. Each additional unit of food now costs more and more clothing. Opportunity cost rises.

⚙️

Resources Are Specialised

Land, machinery, and skills differ across sectors. Not every input is equally productive in every use — which is why the cost of specialisation increases as you push further.

📐

The Slope Gets Steeper

As you move rightward along the PPF (more food, less clothing), the curve steepens — reflecting that each additional unit of food requires giving up more and more clothing.

Result: Increasing opportunity costs give the PPF its characteristic concave (bowed-outward) shape. A straight line would imply that resources are perfectly interchangeable — rarely true in practice.
Beyond the Frontier

Growth Shifts the
Entire Curve Outward

1

More Resources

A larger workforce, new capital, or discovered land moves the PPF outward — the economy can now produce more of both goods simultaneously.

2

Better Technology

Technological progress lets you produce more output from the same inputs. If it improves only one sector, the PPF rotates — expanding on one axis but not the other.

3

Invest Today, Grow Tomorrow

Choosing more capital goods now (machines, education, infrastructure) sacrifices present consumption — but pushes tomorrow's PPF further out. The trade-off is present vs. future prosperity.
