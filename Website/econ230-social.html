ECON 230 · Other-Regarding Preferences

Social
Preferences

Fairness, reciprocity, and cooperation — how other people's outcomes enter our own utility.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Evidence

People regularly sacrifice material gain to achieve fair outcomes.

🤝

Ultimatum Game

Two players split a sum. The proposer offers a split; the responder accepts or rejects. Rejection means both get nothing. Offers below 20–30% are almost universally rejected — people pay to punish unfairness.

🎁

Dictator Game

The proposer simply chooses how to split — no rejection possible. Standard theory predicts keeping everything. In practice, most proposers give 20–30%, with no strategic reason to do so.

🌍

Cross-Cultural Evidence

These patterns have been replicated across dozens of cultures and populations. The specifics vary, but the departure from pure self-interest is universal — it is not a Western artefact.

Ultimatum Game: What the Data Show

The modal offer is close to an even split. Low offers are rejected at substantial personal cost — revealing a preference for punishment of unfairness.

Finding 1 The most common offer is 40–50% of the total. Very few proposers offer the minimum despite it being the rational choice under pure self-interest.
Finding 2 Offers below 20–30% are rejected roughly half the time or more. Responders forfeit real money to punish what they perceive as an unfair offer.
Interpretation Proposers anticipate rejection and offer more. Responders are driven by fairness norms, not just money. Both sides exhibit social preferences.
Dictator Removing the responder's power to reject, proposers still give away 20–30%. This isolates pure altruism and fairness from strategic motives.
Formal Models

Inequality Aversion & Reciprocity

Fehr-Schmidt Model

People dislike both advantageous and disadvantageous inequality. Utility falls when outcomes are unequal — whether you are ahead or behind. The disutility from being behind is larger.

Predictions

Explains ultimatum rejections, dictator giving, wage compression in firms, and resistance to price gouging — all without assuming altruism. Only inequity aversion is needed.

Positive Reciprocity

Respond to generosity with generosity — even when there is no material incentive. Workers who receive unexpectedly high wages work harder. Trust games reveal willingness to reward trust.

Negative Reciprocity

Respond to unkindness with punishment — even at personal cost, even anonymously. Altruistic punishment is common in public goods games and drives cooperation norms in society.

Altruism & Cooperation

Public goods experiments reveal that cooperation is real but fragile — sustained by conditional cooperators who punish free-riders when given the chance.

Finding In public goods games, significant contributions persist — especially early in the game and when subjects can communicate. Pure self-interest predicts zero contribution.
Conditional Most subjects are conditional cooperators — willing to contribute if they believe others will. Free riders exploit this, eroding contributions over time unless punishment is available.
Punishment When subjects can pay to punish free riders — even anonymously — cooperation is sustained at high levels. Altruistic punishment is the mechanism that holds cooperation together.
Markets Social preferences operate in markets too: sellers who price-gouge during shortages face consumer boycotts; employers who impose unfair pay cuts see effort decline beyond what economics predicts.
Why This Matters

Social preferences shape markets, organisations, and policy.

Labour Markets

Workers respond to perceived fairness of wages. Pay cuts below a reference wage trigger effort reductions beyond what efficiency wage theory predicts. Gift exchange is real.

Contracting

Explicit contracts can crowd out intrinsic motivation — reducing performance. Monitoring workers signals distrust, undermining the cooperative relationship. Less is sometimes more.

Tax Compliance

People are more likely to pay taxes when they believe others are complying and when they trust the government to use funds fairly. Compliance is a social norm, not just a calculation.

Price Fairness

Sellers who raise prices during a shortage face consumer backlash even when supply and demand justify it. The concept of a "fair price" constrains markets in ways standard models miss.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

Tipping at a Restaurant You'll Never Return To

You've eaten. The bill is paid. You're in a city you'll never visit again. The waiter can do nothing more for you.

Standard economics says: tip nothing. No future relationship. No material benefit. Zero.

But most people tip anyway — because the waiter worked hard, and fairness matters to us, even when no one is watching and there's nothing to gain. That's a social preference.

"
Humans are conditionally cooperative social animals. We are not purely selfish — but not purely altruistic either. We care about fairness, reward generosity, and punish unkindness at personal cost. These are not departures from rationality. They are rational responses to a social world where reputation, reciprocity, and trust are economically valuable.
