Heuristics
& Biases

How mental shortcuts shape judgment — and where they systematically go wrong.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

Kahneman & Tversky · Thinking, Fast and Slow

Two systems govern how
we think and decide.

⚡

System 1 — Fast

Automatic, effortless, and always on. Generates intuitions, impressions, and quick judgments. Runs on heuristics — mental shortcuts that work well most of the time.

🔍

System 2 — Slow

Deliberate, effortful, and limited in capacity. Engages for hard problems, calculations, and override of impulse. Lazy by design — it defers to System 1 whenever possible.

🎯

Where Biases Come From

Biases arise when System 1 shortcuts are applied outside the environments for which they work. The errors are predictable because they follow directly from the heuristic used.

Representativeness Heuristic

Judge probability by how much something resembles your mental prototype — how representative it is of a category.

Rule If it looks like a duck and quacks like a duck, it's probably a duck. Representativeness is a useful default — but it ignores base rates.
Linda Linda is 31, single, outspoken, and concerned with social justice. Which is more probable — that she is a bank teller, or a bank teller and a feminist?
Fallacy Most people choose "bank teller and feminist" — but a conjunction can never be more probable than either part alone. Representativeness overrides logic.
Also Base rate neglect: when given a vivid description, people ignore the background frequency of the category entirely.

Availability Heuristic

Judge the frequency or probability of an event by how easily examples come to mind.

Rule If you can quickly recall many examples, it feels frequent. If examples are hard to retrieve, it feels rare.
Problem Ease of recall is driven by vividness, recency, and media coverage — not actual frequency. Dramatic events feel more common than they are.
Example Plane crashes vs. car crashes: people fear flying more than driving, despite car travel being far more dangerous per kilometre. Crashes are vivid and memorable.
Also Overreaction to terrorism, underestimation of heart disease, and misjudgement of investment risk all trace partly to availability effects.

Anchoring Effects

An initial number — even an arbitrary one — exerts a powerful pull on subsequent judgments. Adjustment away from the anchor is always insufficient.

Classic Kahneman & Tversky spun a rigged wheel (10 or 65) and asked the % of African UN members. Those anchored at 10 guessed ~25%; those at 65 guessed ~45%.
Mechanism The anchor activates related information in memory, and people adjust from it — but stop too soon, leaving estimates biased toward the starting point.
Real world Legal sentencing, salary negotiation, real estate listing prices, medical diagnosis, and contract negotiations all show robust anchoring effects.
Key The anchor need not be plausible or even relevant. Being told to consider any number before estimating is enough to shift the final judgment.
Two More Core Biases

Confirmation Bias & Overconfidence

Confirmation Bias

We seek out, interpret, and remember information in a way that confirms what we already believe. Disconfirming evidence is discounted or ignored.

Why It Matters

Beliefs become self-reinforcing and sticky. Persistent disagreement survives even when all parties have access to the same data. Common in markets, politics, and medicine.

Calibration Overconfidence

People's 90% confidence intervals are too narrow — when asked for a range they are 90% sure contains the true value, they are typically right only about 50% of the time.

Planning Fallacy

People systematically underestimate how long tasks will take and how much they will cost. Projects run late and over budget almost universally — and we are surprised every time.

Connection: Confirmation bias and overconfidence often interact — we are overconfident in beliefs precisely because we have only searched for confirming evidence.
REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

The Car Dealership Anchor

The salesperson shows you a car and says: "Was $45,000 — today only $32,000!"

Your brain screams "bargain!" But should the original $45,000 matter at all if the car is only worth $28,000? The high sticker price was placed there specifically to make $32,000 feel cheap. That's the anchor. You know you should ignore it — but you can't.
