How central banks use interest rates to manage inflation, employment, and economic growth.

The Big Picture

The central bank manages the economy
through interest rates.

🏦

The Central Bank

In Canada, the Bank of Canada conducts monetary policy. It operates independently from the government — insulated from short-term political pressure to deliver long-run price stability.

🎯

The Mandate

The Bank of Canada's primary goal is to keep inflation low, stable, and predictable. It also supports sustained economic growth and full employment, but price stability comes first.

🔧

The Primary Tool

The main instrument is the overnight policy rate — the interest rate at which major banks lend to each other overnight. Changing this rate ripples through the entire economy.

The Policy Rate

The overnight rate is the Bank of Canada's signal to the entire financial system. When it moves, borrowing costs everywhere follow.

How Banks borrow from each other overnight to manage daily cash needs. The Bank of Canada sets the target for this rate.
Chain Overnight rate → Bank prime rate → Mortgage rates, business loans, consumer credit across the economy.
Signal Each rate change sends a clear message to households, firms, and markets about where the economy is heading.
Schedule The Bank of Canada meets eight times a year to announce its rate decision. Markets watch these meetings closely.
The Two Directions

Expansionary vs. Contractionary Policy

Expansionary (Loose)
Rate ↓
Used when: recession · slow growth · high unemployment
↑Borrowing becomes cheaper
↑Consumer spending rises
↑Business investment expands
↑GDP and employment grow
Contractionary (Tight)
Rate ↑
Used when: high inflation · overheating economy
↓Borrowing becomes more expensive
↓Consumer spending falls
↓Business investment contracts
↓Inflation cools

The Target

2% Inflation Target

The Bank of Canada aims to keep inflation at 2% — the midpoint of a 1–3% control band.

Why 2%? A small positive rate buffers against deflation, which is far more damaging to an economy than mild inflation.

Control band (1–3%): Inflation above 3% triggers tightening; below 1% triggers easing. The band gives room for shocks.

Anchored expectations: If everyone believes inflation will stay near 2%, they set wages and prices accordingly — making the target self-fulfilling. Credibility is everything.
