Why strategic outcomes deviate from Nash equilibrium — fairness, trust, and social preferences in action.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

Why Nash Equilibrium Often Fails

Standard game theory predicts that rational, self-interested players will reach Nash equilibrium. In repeated experiments with real people, they reliably don't — and the deviations are systematic, not random.

Nash Prediction Nash equilibrium assumes all players maximise their own material payoff, given their beliefs about others. The equilibrium is the strategy from which no player can unilaterally improve their own outcome.
What People Actually Do In ultimatum games, people reject unfair offers — even at cost to themselves. In public goods games, they contribute above zero. In trust games, they trust strangers and reciprocate. Nash predicts none of this.
Why People have social preferences — they care about fairness, reciprocity, and relative payoffs, not just their own material gain. These preferences are stable, significant, and predictable in direction.
The Implication Strategic environments must be designed with real human motivations in mind — not just material incentives. Social norms, framing, and reputation all shape outcomes as powerfully as monetary payoffs.

The Ultimatum Game

The simplest and most famous behavioural game. A proposer splits $10 with a responder — who can accept or reject. If rejected, both receive nothing.

Nash Prediction A rational, self-interested proposer offers the minimum positive amount ($0.01). A rational responder accepts anything positive — since something is better than nothing. The equilibrium is highly unequal.
Reality Average offers are 40–45% of the total. Offers below 20–25% are rejected roughly half the time. Proposers anticipate this and offer more. Both sides behave as if fairness matters.
Why Reject? Responders forgo real money to punish unfairness. This is costly punishment — a violation of self-interest. It signals negative reciprocity: people will enforce norms even at personal cost.
Cross-Cultural Henrich et al. (2001) found the ultimatum game produces different average offers across 15 small-scale societies — but the pattern of fairness concerns and costly punishment is universal. Norms vary; reciprocity doesn't.
Cooperation and Free-Riding

The Public Goods Game

🏛️

The Setup

Each player receives 10 tokens and can contribute any amount to a group fund. Contributions are multiplied (usually by 2×) and split equally — making free-riding individually rational but collectively destructive.

📊

What Happens

Nash equilibrium: contribute zero. Reality: initial contributions average 40–60% of endowment. But cooperation unravels — contributions fall toward zero as the game is repeated, as conditional cooperators defect in response to free-riders.

⚡

Conditional Cooperation

Fischbacher, Gächter, and Fehr (2001): most people are conditional cooperators — they match the average contribution of others. A minority are free-riders. Free-riders unravel cooperation by pulling averages down.

The Trust Game

Berg, Dickhaut, and McCabe (1995). An investor sends any amount (multiplied 3×) to a trustee, who decides how much to return. Standard theory predicts: send nothing, return nothing.

Nash Prediction A self-interested trustee will return zero — since returning money is costly. Anticipating this, a rational investor sends zero. Backward induction yields no trade and no gains from trust.
Reality Investors send on average 50% of their endowment. Trustees return roughly 35–45% of what they receive — often returning more than the original amount. Trust is rewarded with reciprocity.
Positive Reciprocity Investors trust strangers. Trustees feel obligated to repay — not because they must, but because of positive reciprocity. The relationship generates mutual gains that standard theory says cannot exist.
Institutional Implication Markets, employment relationships, and contracts all depend on trust beyond what formal enforcement can sustain. Understanding reciprocity is essential for designing institutions that function in the real world.
Repeated Interaction

Learning and Bounded Rationality in Repeated Games

Directional Learning

Players move in the direction of strategies that gave higher payoffs in the past — they don't optimise fully, but they improve. Over time, this can produce Nash-like outcomes even without full rationality.

Reinforcement Learning

Strategies that led to good outcomes in the past are more likely to be chosen in the future. This simple rule produces systematic dynamics — including rapid defection in public goods games as cooperation fails.

Reputation and Signalling

In repeated games, players build reputations. Cooperation can be sustained not by altruism but by the threat of punishment in future rounds. Tit-for-tat strategies outperform pure defection over time.

End-Game Unravelling

In finitely repeated games, cooperation collapses near the end by backward induction. In practice, cooperation often persists longer — because people are uncertain about others' types, or simply don't backward induct fully.
