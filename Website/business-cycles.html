Business
Cycles

The recurring expansions and contractions that all market economies experience — and the policy tools used to smooth them.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Big Picture

Recurring fluctuations in real GDP
around its long-run potential.

📈

Expansion

Real GDP is rising, unemployment is falling, and business confidence is high. The economy is operating closer to — or above — its full-employment potential. Also called a recovery or boom.

📉

Recession

Real GDP falls for at least two consecutive quarters. Unemployment rises, investment collapses, and consumer spending contracts. Also called a contraction or downturn.

📏

Potential GDP

The level of output an economy can sustainably produce at full employment — without generating inflation. Business cycles are fluctuations around this trend, not permanent departures from it.

Four Phases of the Business Cycle

Every cycle follows the same recognisable pattern — though the length and severity of each phase varies enormously.

Peak The high point. Real GDP is at its maximum before the economy begins to turn down. Unemployment is at its lowest; inflation risks are highest.
Contraction GDP falls, unemployment rises, investment collapses. If it lasts two or more quarters, it is officially called a recession.
Trough The low point. GDP stops falling. Unemployment peaks. This is the turning point where contraction ends and recovery begins.
Expansion GDP rises, jobs return, confidence rebuilds. The economy climbs back toward — and sometimes beyond — potential output.
Reading the Cycle

Cyclical Indicators

📈 Procyclical — Move with GDP

Consumer spending — rises in expansions, falls in recessions
Business investment — most volatile procyclical variable
Corporate profits & tax revenues — both swell when GDP grows
Industrial production & retail sales — rise and fall with the cycle

📉 Countercyclical — Move against GDP

Unemployment rate — rises in recessions, falls in expansions
Government transfers — EI and social assistance spike in downturns
Budget deficit — widens in recessions (automatic stabilisers)
Bankruptcy filings — surge when GDP contracts

⏩ Leading Indicators

Change before the cycle turns. Examples: housing starts, stock prices, new orders for capital goods, consumer confidence index. Used to forecast turning points.

⏪ Lagging Indicators

Change after the cycle turns. The unemployment rate is the classic example — it keeps rising even after GDP has started to recover.

↔ Coincident Indicators

Change at the same time as the cycle. Real GDP, employment, and personal income are coincident — they confirm what the economy is doing right now.

What Causes Business Cycles?

Unexpected shocks to either aggregate demand or aggregate supply disrupt the economy's equilibrium and trigger a cycle.

Positive Demand Shock

↑ Spending surge

Consumer confidence boom, large fiscal stimulus, export surge. GDP rises above potential → inflationary pressure builds.

Negative Demand Shock

↓ Spending collapse

Financial crisis, housing market crash, pandemic shock. Consumer and investor confidence collapses, triggering recession.

Positive Supply Shock

↑ Productivity gain

Major technological advance, falling commodity prices. Output rises and prices fall simultaneously — a welcome combination.

Negative Supply Shock

↓ Cost spike

Oil price surge, supply chain disruption, natural disaster. Output falls while prices rise — stagflation, the worst of both worlds.

Real business cycles: Some economists argue cycles reflect rational responses to technology shocks, not market failures. Most mainstream analysis, however, emphasises demand-side shocks and sticky wages as primary drivers.
The Policy Diagnostic

The Output Gap

Output gap = (Actual GDP − Potential GDP) ÷ Potential GDP × 100%. It tells policymakers not just whether the economy is growing, but whether it is growing at a sustainable pace.

Negative output gap (actual < potential): Unemployment above its natural rate. Unused capacity. Inflation below target. Calls for expansionary fiscal or monetary policy.

Positive output gap (actual > potential): Economy overheating. Labour markets tight. Inflation above target. Calls for contractionary policy to cool demand.

Zero output gap: Actual GDP at potential. Full employment without inflation pressure. The goal of macroeconomic stabilisation policy.
