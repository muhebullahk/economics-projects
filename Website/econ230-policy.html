How behavioural insights are transforming health, education, tax policy, and institutional design.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Policy Gap

Standard policy tools assume rational agents — behavioural policy works with real ones.

🚧

Where Standard Tools Fall Short

Information campaigns, price incentives, and mandates all assume people respond logically. They miss present bias, inattention, friction, and social norms — the forces that actually drive behaviour.

🛠️

The Behavioural Toolkit

Smart defaults, simplified processes, social norm messages, commitment devices, and salient reminders. These tools work with cognitive constraints rather than assuming they don't exist.

📐

Evidence-Based Design

Behavioural policy is built on randomised controlled trials — field experiments that directly measure impact. The UK Behavioural Insights Team pioneered this approach at government scale.

Health Behaviour

The gap between knowing and doing is nowhere more costly than in health. Present bias, optimism bias, and friction prevent people acting on knowledge they already have.

Vaccination Switching from opt-in to opt-out flu vaccination in workplaces raises uptake from ~40% to over 70%. Framing as protecting others (not just yourself) increases acceptance further.
Smoking Financial commitment contracts — where smokers put down a deposit that is returned only if they quit — are among the most effective smoking cessation tools. Sophisticated smokers seek them out.
Screening Cancer screening take-up rises substantially when letters include a specific appointment time rather than asking patients to call to schedule. Removing the step removes the friction that causes delay.
Obesity Smaller default plate sizes, healthier cafeteria layouts, and displaying calorie counts at the point of choice all reduce consumption — without banning any food or changing a single price.
Education & Finance

Complexity and present bias are the dominant barriers — not information.

University Access

FAFSA simplification raised US college enrolment significantly among low-income students. The barrier was the application form, not the cost of college. Reducing form length from dozens of pages to a few questions made the difference.

Financial Literacy

Financial education has surprisingly weak effects on savings behaviour. The barrier is not lack of knowledge — it's complexity and procrastination. Automatic savings programmes consistently outperform education interventions.

Save More Tomorrow

Thaler and Benartzi's programme lets workers pre-commit to directing future pay raises toward savings. Take-up is high because the commitment is future-dated — it doesn't hurt now. Savings rates quadrupled in field trials.

Student Debt

Simplified income-contingent repayment enrolment, personalised debt summaries, and automatic income-verification dramatically increase enrolment in programmes that prevent default — among borrowers who were already eligible.

Tax Compliance & Behavioural Public Policy

Tax compliance is far higher than the standard rational-calculator model predicts — and social norms are a key reason why. Field experiments show precisely how compliance messages can be improved.

Standard Model Allingham and Sandmo: people evade until the expected penalty × audit probability makes evasion unprofitable. In practice, compliance is far higher — most people don't think about audits at all.
Social Norms HMRC field experiments showed that adding "9 out of 10 people in your area have already paid" to late-payment letters raised on-time payment rates by ~5 percentage points — worth tens of millions of pounds in cash flow.
Moral Appeals Messages emphasising that taxes fund hospitals, schools, and public services also improve compliance — measurably, though less strongly than social norm messages. People respond to the social meaning of payment.
Simplification Pre-populated tax returns — where the government fills in what it already knows — dramatically raise compliance rates and reduce costly errors. Used in Scandinavia; estimated to raise US compliance by billions if adopted.
Beyond Individual Interventions

Behavioural insights reshape how institutions and systems are designed.

Pension Systems

Opt-out auto-enrolment transformed retirement savings in the UK, US, and Australia. Participation rates jumped from 40–50% to 85–95% with no change to contribution rates or tax incentives — only the default changed.

Healthcare Systems

Personalised appointment letters, pre-booked slots, and simplified booking processes each raise screening and vaccination rates substantially. These are systemic changes — built into how the health system operates, not one-off campaigns.

Criminal Justice

Implementation intention planning — "if X, then I will do Y" — combined with structured check-ins reduces reoffending. Commitment contracts for community service orders improve completion rates. Simple structure changes outcomes.

Employment Services

Job centres using specific implementation plans ("I will apply to these three firms on Tuesday between 10 and 12") produce significantly better employment outcomes than general motivational interviews. Specificity produces action.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

One Sentence That Raised £Millions

The UK tax authority sent two versions of a late-payment reminder letter. The only difference: one added the line

"9 out of 10 people in your area have already paid their tax."

That single sentence raised on-time payment by ~5 percentage points — generating tens of millions of pounds in earlier revenue. No new law. No penalty increase. Just a social norm.
