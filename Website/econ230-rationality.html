Limits of
Rationality

Why the standard model of rational choice breaks down — and what replaces it.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Standard Model

Economics assumes a perfectly
rational decision-maker.

📋

Well-Defined Preferences

Agents have complete, consistent, and transitive preferences over all possible outcomes. They always know what they want.

🎲

Expected Utility

Under uncertainty, agents maximise expected utility — weighting outcomes by their probabilities and choosing the option with the highest weighted value.

⚙️

Full Optimisation

Given all available information, agents compute the best possible choice and execute it — every time, at no cognitive cost.

Expected Utility Theory

The formal language of rational choice under risk, developed by Von Neumann and Morgenstern (1944).

Rule For each risky option, multiply each outcome's value by its probability and sum the results. Choose the option with the highest expected utility.
Example Gamble A: 50% chance of $100, 50% chance of $0. EU = 0.5 × u($100) + 0.5 × u($0).
Key The utility function u(·) captures risk preferences. A concave u means risk aversion — a sure $50 is preferred to the gamble above.
Axioms The model rests on four axioms: completeness, transitivity, continuity, and independence. Violate any one — and EU breaks down.
Empirical Evidence

The model fails systematically
in real-world settings.

Allais Paradox

Most people violate the independence axiom of EU theory when choosing between carefully designed lotteries — a finding replicated worldwide.

Loss Aversion

Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains. People refuse fair gambles — a systematic departure from expected utility maximisation.

Framing Effects

Identical choices yield different decisions depending on how they are presented — as gains or losses, percentages or frequencies.

Present Bias

People over-weight immediate rewards relative to future ones — beyond what standard discounting predicts — leading to self-control failures.

Pattern: These are not random errors. They are systematic, predictable, and stable across populations — which is what makes them economically important.

Bounded Rationality

Herbert Simon (1955) argued that people are rational — but only within limits set by their cognitive capacity, time, and available information.

Cognitive limits: Working memory, attention, and processing speed are finite. Full optimisation is computationally impossible for most real decisions.

Information limits: We rarely have complete information. Gathering it is costly in time and effort. Decisions are made under genuine uncertainty.

Satisficing, not optimising: Rather than finding the best option, people search until they find one that is "good enough" — and stop there.

Heuristics: Mental shortcuts — rules of thumb — substitute for full computation. They are often effective, but create predictable biases.

So What?

Why this matters for economics
and policy.

Markets Can Amplify Mistakes

When many people share the same bias — such as overconfidence or present bias — markets do not automatically correct it. Errors can persist and compound.

Standard Policy May Fail

Policies designed for fully rational agents often do not work as predicted. Information campaigns, price signals, and incentives all interact with cognitive limits.

Behavioural Policy Works

Nudges, defaults, and choice architecture exploit known biases to improve outcomes — without restricting freedom. Pension auto-enrolment is a canonical example.

Better Models

Prospect theory, hyperbolic discounting, and dual-process models replace EU theory with frameworks that fit the data — and generate testable predictions.

REAL-LIFE EXAMPLE

The Cereal Aisle

A perfectly rational shopper facing 47 cereals would read every label, calculate cost-per-calorie, and pick the optimal box. Nobody does this.

Instead, you grab the familiar brand at eye level — something "good enough" — and move on. That's satisficing, not optimising. Bounded rationality, not laziness.
