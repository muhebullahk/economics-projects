The Framework

Government spending and taxation
as macroeconomic tools.

🏗️

Government Spending (G)

Public spending on goods, services, infrastructure, and transfers (employment insurance, social assistance) directly injects money into the economy and boosts aggregate demand.

💸

Taxation (T)

Taxes withdraw income from households and firms. Tax cuts leave more money in private hands; tax increases reduce disposable income and dampen spending across the economy.

🎯

The Goal

Fiscal policy aims to stabilise the business cycle — stimulating demand during recessions, cooling it during booms — while supporting long-run growth and employment.

The Spending Multiplier

$1 of government spending creates more than $1 of GDP. Each round of spending becomes someone else's income.

Round 1 Govt spends $1B on a highway. Construction workers earn $1B in income.
Round 2 Workers spend $0.80B at local stores (MPC = 0.8). Store owners earn $0.80B.
Round 3 Store owners spend $0.64B. And so on, each round smaller than the last.
Multiplier 1 ÷ (1 − MPC) = 1 ÷ 0.2 = 5. One dollar of spending creates five dollars of GDP.
Two Directions

Expansionary vs. Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Expansionary (Loose)
G ↑  or  T ↓
Used when: recession · high unemployment · slow growth
↑Government spending rises — public works, transfers
↑Tax cuts leave more in households' pockets
↑Aggregate demand and GDP rise
↑Employment recovers
Contractionary (Tight)
G ↓  or  T ↑
Used when: high inflation · overheating economy
↓Government spending is cut or held flat
↓Tax increases reduce disposable income
↓Aggregate demand cools
↓Inflation pressures ease

Automatic Stabilisers

Some fiscal tools respond automatically to the economy — no legislation needed. They cushion downturns and cool booms without a single new vote.

📉 Recession

↓GDP and employment fall
↓Tax revenues fall (less income to tax)
↑EI and social assistance rise automatically
↑Net: government injects spending, cushioning the fall
⚖️
Auto-
matic

📈 Expansion

↑GDP and employment rise
↑Tax revenues rise (more income to tax)
↓EI and transfer payments fall automatically
↓Net: government withdraws stimulus, cooling the boom
Key advantage: Automatic stabilisers act immediately — no implementation lag, no political debate. They soften the business cycle in both directions.
The Fiscal Balance

Budget, Deficit & National Debt

When spending exceeds revenue, governments run deficits. Deficits accumulate into national debt.

Budget surplus: Revenue > Spending. The government is paying down debt or saving. Typically achieved in strong economic periods.

Budget deficit: Spending > Revenue. The government borrows by issuing bonds, adding to the national debt. Common during recessions.

National debt concern: Persistent deficits cause debt to grow faster than GDP — potentially crowding out private investment and limiting future fiscal room.
