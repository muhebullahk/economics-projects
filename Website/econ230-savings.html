Intertemporal Choice
& Savings Behaviour

Why people systematically undersave — and how mental accounting shapes every financial decision.

Prof. Muhebullah Karimzada · UNBC Economics

The Savings Shortfall

People consistently save less than they know they should. This is not a knowledge problem — it is a self-control problem rooted in present bias and the difficulty of making financial decisions under complexity.

The Gap The median household nearing retirement holds savings sufficient to replace only 30–40% of pre-retirement income. Surveys show people believe 70–80% is needed. They know the target. They don't hit it.
Why Present bias makes retirement saving especially hard: the cost is immediate (foregone consumption today) while the benefit is distant and abstract (future security in decades). The future self is deeply discounted.
Intention Gap Laibson et al. (1998): most people report they should save "more than I currently do." The gap between stated intention and actual saving is large, persistent, and cannot be explained by income constraints alone.
Complexity Complex pension menus, uncertain return projections, and confusing paperwork raise cognitive costs — making procrastination the path of least resistance, even for people who genuinely intend to act.

Mental Accounting

Richard Thaler's theory: people divide money into separate mental "buckets" — and treat each differently, despite money being perfectly fungible.

Fungibility Fails Standard theory: a dollar is a dollar regardless of source. Reality: a tax refund is spent differently from earned income of the same amount. The source changes its psychological meaning.
MPC Differs The marginal propensity to consume out of windfall income (bonus, refund, lottery) is far lower than out of regular income — even though the purchasing power is identical. Windfalls feel like wealth to save.
Credit Card Puzzle Many households hold both high-interest credit card debt (18–22%) and low-yield savings (1–2%) simultaneously. Under standard theory this is dominated. Mental accounting keeps the accounts separate.
House Money Thaler's "house money effect": investors take more risk with market gains than with their initial capital. Gains are coded as a separate, more expendable mental account — so risk feels less costly.
The Fix

Automatic savings programmes work — because they remove the need for willpower.

📋

Auto-Enrolment

Switching pension enrolment from opt-in to opt-out raises participation from ~40% to 85–95%. Inertia is powerful — people stay with the default. Setting the right default harnesses inertia constructively.

🔮

Save More Tomorrow

Thaler and Benartzi's SMarT programme: workers pre-commit to directing future pay raises into pension savings. The commitment is future-dated — it costs the present self nothing. In trials, savings rates quadrupled.

🔒

Commitment Accounts

Locked savings accounts — where funds cannot be withdrawn until a target date — dramatically raise savings in field experiments. Sophisticated agents actively seek them to constrain their future selves.

Beyond Savings

Mental Accounting in Debt and Spending

Credit Card Debt

Minimum payments on high-interest cards while maintaining low-yield savings accounts. Mentally, "debt" and "savings" are separate — the arbitrage is invisible. Standard theory predicts immediate payoff.

Windfall Spending

Tax refunds and bonuses are treated as "extra" money — mentally assigned to a savings bucket — and spent more slowly than equivalent regular income. The fungibility of money fails in practice.

Sunk Costs

People finish meals they don't enjoy because they paid; hold losing stocks to avoid realising losses already locked in. The money is gone either way — but mental accounting tracks it as still mattering.

Payment Decoupling

Credit cards decouple payment from consumption, reducing the "pain of paying" and raising spending. Cash registers higher pain than card; card registers higher pain than subscription. Salience matters.

REAL-WORLD EXAMPLE

The SMarT Programme

Thaler and Benartzi partnered with a manufacturing firm in the late 1990s. They offered employees a simple deal: you don't have to save more today — but the next time you get a pay raise, agree in advance to direct half of it into your pension.

Because the commitment was future-dated, the present self lost nothing in the moment. Present bias was sidestepped, not overcome. Within two years, average savings rates among participants rose from 3.5% to 13.6% — a fourfold increase with no mandate or subsidy.
