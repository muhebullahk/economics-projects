Teaching Philosophy

Approach to economics education

Teaching economics is, to me, an act of storytelling through theory and data. I approach each course as an opportunity to help students see how economic models interpret the world around them. My goal is to cultivate not only an understanding of macroeconomic principles but also an economist's mindset: analytical, empirical, and curious.

My teaching rests on three pillars. First, conceptual clarity: I use intuitive explanations before introducing formal models, so that by the time students encounter the framework they already understand the forces at work. Second, application: I integrate Canadian and international data in every course, drawing on Statistics Canada, the IMF, and the World Bank. Assignments include empirical projects, policy briefs, and simulations — for example, groups in my Money and Banking course simulate a Bank of Canada meeting, using live inflation and GDP data to justify interest rate decisions. Third, engagement: I foster discussion-based learning where students take the role of policymakers or analysts, building both deeper understanding and communication skills.

I make active use of technology to strengthen student learning, including custom Excel and Python-based simulations for topics such as the money multiplier, output gap analysis, and exchange rate adjustments. Inclusivity is central to my approach: I adapt materials to varied learning backgrounds and maintain an open-door policy for mentorship, graduate school advising, and one-on-one support.

Over the course of my teaching career I have supported students who have gone on to graduate programmes at institutions including the University of Toronto, Stanford, MIT, and Oxford.
